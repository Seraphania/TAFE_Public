### Question 1
Complete  

*There are four key information-gathering techniques - **Observation**, surveys, interviews and focus groups.*  

*Research and then explain, in your own words, what **observation** is and what the advantages and disadvantages are to using it to collect data.*  

**Selected Answer:**  
Observation is gathering information by directly observing behavior, this may be be participant observation (interacting with the subject(s) of the observation) or unobtrusive observation, where the observer does not interact with the the subject(s).

Observation can be useful for gathering certain types of real-time data, for example candid feedback about a dining experience or observing animal behavior. It can also allow a researcher to gain additional perspective or information that they may not have otherwise thought of if they are interacting with a subject.

Observation is not always a suitable method for information gathering however; observing a subject in some situations may make them behave differently than they otherwise might if they were not observed, or aware of being observed - and it may be unethical to do so without them being informed and giving consent. Because observation is in real-time, it may be difficult for a researcher to collect all relevant information at once, which may mean that some information is missed, and if a researcher is taking notes on observed behavior, they may inadvertently introduce their own biases, or misunderstand context.

### Question 2
Complete  

*There are four key information-gathering techniques - Observation, **surveys**, interviews and focus groups.*  

*Research and then explain, in your own words, what **surveys** are and what the advantages and disadvantages are to using it to collect data.*


**Selected Answer:**  
Surveys are a very common use for collecting information. They are often used to gather quantitative information of the type "On a scale of 1-10, how much do you agree with the following...", however they may also be used to gather more qualitative data, for example what is your opinion on x?". An example of this is the Census conducted by the Australian government every 5 years.

Surveys are effective because they can often be administered very widely because the questions are typically standardised and repeated exactly for each participant.  This allowing for more samples, and therefore better statistical validity, they are also relatively  inexpensive to perform; it is not necessary to pay for an observer. In addition, the data is already fairly standardised due to the nature of the test (responses are likely to fall into a limited range of options) statistical analysis can be preformed quickly because the data doesn't require very much cleaning.

The uniformity of surveys, while an advantage in many ways may also be a disadvantage, they do not allow the collection of rich answers and if not well designed the data may be meaningless if insufficient options for responses are provided. Because surveys are typically filled out by individual subjects, often without oversight, the answers may be similarly subjective, whereas a single observer or measurer might be more consistent.  Surveys are generally good for narrow information gathering, but are less effective for more qualitative studies such as ecological or biodiversity modelling.

Source: https://researchevaluationconsulting.com/4-data-collection-methods/

*There are four key information-gathering techniques - Observation, surveys, **interviews** and focus groups.*  

*Research and then explain, in your own words, what **interviews** are and what the advantages and disadvantages are to using it to collect data.*

**Selected Answer:**  
Interviews are often used to gather qualitative data; thoughts, feelings and opinions, rather than quantitative data such as measurements. There is quite a bit of scope for them to be focused or open ended. They are typically conducted one-on one rather than in groups.

Interviews are an excellent method for gathering sensitive information provided the interviewer can build a rapport with the subject, they also allow a researcher to follow up on a topic in real time, allowing additional data to be gathered as appropriate on an ad-hoc basis.

The information gathered from interviews however may be highly subjective and dependant on the ability of the interviewer to encourage the subject to discuss the topic being researched.

### Question 4
Complete  

*There are four key information-gathering techniques - Observation, surveys, interviews and **focus groups**.*  

*Research and then explain, in your own words, what **focus groups** are and what the advantages and disadvantages are to using it to collect data.*

**Selected Answer:**  
Focus Groups are similar to interviews in terms of gathering qualitative data, however they are performed with a group rather than individually.

As with interviews, focus groups are excellent for expanding further on a topic, with the added benefit of being able to get data from more people simultaneously, also having multiple respondents simultaneously may help the participants "open up" and think of additional information based on what other people have said.

Focus groups are less suitable for gathering certain types of data (such as sensitive information), and care may be used conducting a focus group as there is a risk of group dynamics affecting the responses given by participants, which may cause biases in the information.

Source: https://researchevaluationconsulting.com/4-data-collection-methods/

### Question 6
Complete  
 
*A common smartphone is full of interesting sensors that can be used to collect data via observation (albeit observation by a computer). Explain what each of the following measure as well as a practical use for it.*
1. Accelerometer.
2. Gyroscope.
3. LiDAR (Not actually shipping in a smartphone yet but it's coming this year).
4. Proximity sensor.
5. Ambient light sensor.
6. Barometer.


**Selected Answer:**  
1. Accelerometer:
   Measures the proper acceleration of an object (the phone), this assists in determining how it is moving in space, this information can be used for gestures, determining location, measuring distance traveled, among other things.

2. Gyroscope:
   Measures orientation and angular velocity. This assists im many of the same applications as the accelerometer above by providing orientation information.

3. LiDAR (Not actually shipping in a smartphone yet but it's coming this year):
   Light Detection and Ranging - uses measurements of light to measure space. This can be used for 3D mapping and accurate measurement of objects and spaces, as well as enhancing things like augmented reality.

4. Proximity sensor.
   These are able to detect movement near the phone. The information may be used for things like lowering the volume on alarms when a user is nearby.

5. Ambient light sensor.
   Detects the intensity of ambient light. This often adjusts the phones screen brightness for better visibility in different environments, such as increasing screen brightness during the day so it can be seen, and lowering it at night to avoid eye strain.

6. Barometer.
   Barometers measure atmospheric pressure. In phones this can help improve GPS accuracy by providing elevation data, as well as assisting with weather prediction.

### Question 12
Complete  

*There are four key data analysis techniques - descriptive, diagnostic, predictive and **prescriptive**.*

*Research and then explain, in your own words, what **prescriptive** analysis is and give an example where it could be used.*

**Selected Answer:**  
Prescriptive analysis takes predictive analysis a step further, after making a prediction about what is likely to happen, prescriptive analysis might use additional data to make decisions about the best actions to take.

Following on from the weather example above, if the prediction has indicated that conditions suggest a high chance of bushfire, a prescriptive analysis might investigate what the effects of similar bushfires were from a variety of angles; for example impacts on animals, people and infrastructure, as well as what has and hasn't worked well in the past to mitigate these effects.  
With that information a course of action can be chosen with priority given to the most critical areas; such a course might be 
1. Restrict activities likely to trigger a bushfire.
2. Allocate more resources for monitoring for fire outbreaks to improve response times.
3. Prepare people and resources to respond so they can act quickly if needed.
4. Communicate with the public about the increased danger.
